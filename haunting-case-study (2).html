A seasonal event built to drive depth — not just engagement

The Haunting was a limited-time Halloween event integrated directly into the live Call of Duty HQ experience, serving tens of millions of active players across console and PC.

The event introduced two interconnected progression systems that players had to navigate in real-time between matches. Players earned a seasonal currency — Souls — during gameplay, then redeemed it for exclusive rewards in an event store. Simultaneously, they tracked and completed six map-based monster hunt challenges, unlocking a mastery weapon blueprint and unique collectibles as the ultimate reward.

I led the UX across the entire event hub: interaction design, visual system, information architecture, reward visibility, and progression flows — ensuring the experience felt immersive and thematically alive while remaining instantly legible to players who had seconds, not minutes, to parse it.

The season was recognized by the community as one of the strongest live-service events of the year — a direct signal that when UX and narrative reinforce each other, players notice.

02   Problem

Two systems. One event window.
Zero room for confusion.

Call of Duty's live HQ interface is a high-velocity environment. Players spend 45–90 seconds in menus between matches. Any friction — an unclear reward state, a missed progression indicator, an ambiguous CTA — creates drop-off and disengagement from the event entirely.

The Haunting introduced two simultaneous systems that players had to understand quickly: a currency earn loop and a spatial objective system. The core UX problem wasn't making these look good — it was making them feel like one coherent experience, not two tasks to manage.

The hub had to serve three distinct player types simultaneously, each with different goals and attention spans: completionists tracking mastery progress, casual players browsing rewards, and high-frequency players who just wanted to know what they earned after a match.

①
SOULS SYSTEM
Match-earned currency redeemable in an event store for cosmetics, blueprints, and consumables. The earn loop needed to feel rewarding with every session.
②

Technical

05   Research + Insights

Understanding how users navigate under time pressure

Players in live-service ecosystems are not browsing — they're scanning. They operate in 45-second decision windows between matches. Every insight below was derived from behavioral observation and existing engagement data from prior seasonal events.

INSIGHT 01
Scanning Behavior
Users in high-frequency apps develop recognition patterns — not reading patterns.
Design Implication State changes must be visible peripherally. Progress updates can't live buried inside submenus.
INSIGHT 02
Dual Progression Fatigue
When two progress bars coexist, users anchor to the one they're closest to completing.
Design Implication Visual hierarchy must reinforce which system is primary at any given moment — not leave users to decide.
INSIGHT 03
Reward Legibility
Users abandon reward systems they can't mentally model in under 10 seconds.
Design Implication Every reward must surface its earn mechanism before its cost. Show the path before showing the price.
INSIGHT 04
Discovery Behavior
Players explore seasonal content in 3-second decision bursts between queue loading.
Design Implication Every CTA must work in isolation without surrounding context. Don't design for ideal reading order.
06   Design Strategy

One hub. Two systems.
A single mental model.

Rather than treating the Souls currency system and the Monster Hunt as two separate features, I designed them as one cohesive event narrative. Match activity earned Souls. Souls revealed reward targets. Reward targets motivated deeper play — which drove Monster Hunt completion. Every loop reinforced the next.

The UX strategy: design the hub so that both systems felt like progress toward one exciting outcome — the mastery weapon blueprint — rather than two parallel tasks competing for the player's attention.

This meant the event hub had to answer three questions at a glance: What did I earn? What can I unlock? What's left to do? In that order, every time.

08   Interaction Design

Three zones. Every decision annotated.

Each component in the event hub was designed with explicit state logic, annotated for engineering, and validated against the core insight that players operate in 3-second decision windows.

Component 01
Souls Counter Widget
  • Updates in real-time post-match — reinforces the reward loop without requiring navigation
  • "152 SOULS CAPTURED" uses player's specific count — ownership language, not abstract currency
  • CTA arrows below the counter hint at next action without instructing — guided but not patronizing
  • Green color token ties directly to the earn state — players learn green = progress, not taught
Component 02
Reward Grid System
  • Cost shown as trophy count, not abstract number — players understand relative effort at a glance
  • Green text = affordable. Neutral = not yet earned. Locked state preserved player dignity — no red X
  • Card grid mirrors the Battle Pass mental model — zero new UX pattern to learn, leveraging existing habit
  • Mastery side card peeks from the right — always visible, never requiring a sub-navigation to discover
Component 03
Monster Hunt Map Widget
  • 6 monster thumbnails = 6 objectives. One-to-one spatial mapping removes scope ambiguity completely
  • Locked state uses grayed visual outline, not disabled text — visual language, not verbal instruction
  • "VIEW CLASSIFIED INTEL" CTA uses narrative language — clicking feels like gameplay, not admin
  • Widget width constrained to sidebar — doesn't compete with reward grid as the primary attention zone

The map-based layout gives players a geographic mental model of objectives. Monsters are located, not listed — tying the UI directly to the in-game world.
Progressive Disclosure
Challenge details surface only on selection. The overview shows completion state — not mechanics — so players can assess progress in a single glance before clicking deeper.
Mastery as Destination
The mastery blueprint is always visible as the endpoint — a constant motivational anchor that reframes every individual challenge as one step toward the ultimate reward.
Mastery Blueprint Hover State — Reward Reveal
Reward Reveal Moment
The mastery weapon blueprint reveal is treated as a climactic UI moment — full panel focus, weapon showcase, and completion ceremony. Earned moments deserve earned presentation.
Locked State Dignity
Locked items use visual obscuring rather than disabled text. Players understand "not yet" without being told "no" — preserving motivation instead of creating friction.
Progress Persistence
Mastery progress pips are always visible in the panel. Players know exactly how many items they've unlocked toward the mastery reward without navigating to a separate screen.

System Principles
  • All interactive states defined upfront: default, hover, selected, locked, and disabled — zero ambiguity for engineering
  • Components use existing HQ design tokens only — no new color primitives or type styles introduced
  • Event theme applied via color variables and texture overlays exclusively — structure untouched, future seasons reuse freely
  • Every component annotated with state logic and Figma-to-engineering handoff specs before engineering started

12   Collaboration

Cross-functional from brief to ship

Embedded as the sole UX voice in a cross-discipline sprint team — building alignment through annotated prototypes and component specs rather than documentation.

Weeks 1–2
Product
Defined event scope, player flows, and hub information hierarchy with PM team. Established dual-system narrative strategy and player journey mapping.
Weeks 3–4
Engineering
Reviewed feasibility of hub components and interaction state logic. Delivered annotated specs. Engineering parallelize-built while I iterated — no sequential handoff.
Weeks 5–6
Art + Narrative
Aligned UI theme with seasonal visual direction and narrative voice. Ensured the UX language ("Souls Captured," "Classified Intel") matched narrative intent without sacrificing clarity.
Weeks 7–8

Final handoff with component documentation and annotation. State logic validation across console and PC. Real-time iteration on edge cases surfaced during QA pass.
Operated as the only UX voice in a team of engineers, artists, and narrative designers. Built alignment through annotated prototypes and component-level specifications rather than lengthy documentation — enabling parallel workstreams and eliminating the traditional design-then-hand-off bottleneck.
13   Reflection

What I carry forward from this project

Three lenses I use to evaluate any live-service or SaaS product design problem since shipping The Haunting.

What Worked
The narrative arc strategy Treating both systems as one event story gave the hub a through-line that players followed intuitively — without a tutorial or explanation.
Component-first delivery Engineering could build in parallel because specs were complete before screens were final. Faster ship, fewer revision cycles.
Progressive disclosure on mastery Keeping the blueprint visible but partially obscured created sustained aspiration — players returned not because they were told to, but because they wanted to.
What I'd Change
Earlier map alignment We iterated more than necessary on Monster Hunt pin placement and locked state logic. Spatial systems need dedicated alignment sessions, not inline review.
Prototype the earn moment The static Souls counter misses the satisfaction of the earn animation. Given more time, I'd have prototyped the post-match currency update in motion — even a simple one.
Player type segmentation The hub served all three player types but wasn't explicitly designed to. Explicit persona-to-zone mapping from week one would have accelerated alignment with PM.
What I Carry Forward
Information hierarchy is the job Not the art direction. Not the motion. The question "what does a user need to know first?" should precede every design decision.
Seasonal UX is retention design Every interaction in a live-service event is a reason to return — or a reason not to. The design shapes behavior far more than the reward itself.
Constraints accelerate clarity The tightest constraints — 8 weeks, existing components only, no new nav — produced the clearest design. Constraints are a design tool, not a limitation.
